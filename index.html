Section 01

Fruit & Vegetable Selection

Four candidates were chosen to cover both climacteric and non-climacteric physiology, giving a full picture of the coating's mechanisms.

🍓
Strawberry
Fragaria × ananassa
Non-Climacteric
Extremely perishable. Thin epidermis, no rind. Primary target for Botrytis cinerea (grey mould). Fastest water loss. Must be procured at full red maturity (≥75% red surface).
⚠ Fail threshold: 2.5–3.0% weight loss
PMC5224550 – Chitosan on strawberry postharvest quality
🍌
Banana
Musa acuminata
Climacteric
Strong climacteric ethylene burst drives rapid peel browning and softening. Procure at Stage 2–3 (green with yellow tips). Excellent visible ripening proxy — colour change is unmistakable without equipment.
⚠ Fail threshold: ~5% weight loss + stage 5 peel
MDPI 2624-7402 – Edible coatings fruit shelf life
🍅
Cherry Tomato
Solanum lycopersicum var. cerasiforme
Climacteric
Continuous visible ripening (green→red) via lycopene synthesis tied to ethylene & O₂. Procure at Breaker–Turning stage (10–30% colour change). High susceptibility to Penicillium.
⚠ Fail threshold: 5.0–6.0% weight loss
ASHS – Chitosan coating to extend tomato storage life
🫑
Bell Pepper
Capsicum annuum
Non-Climacteric
Non-climacteric but sensitive to moisture loss and chilling. Procure firm, fully coloured (red or green). Similar failure mode to zucchini: surface shrivelling, pitting. Good chilling stress test at 4°C.
⚠ Fail threshold: ~4.0–5.0% weight loss
UC Davis Postharvest – Bell Pepper facts
Section 02

How to Prepare the Coating Solutions

You will prepare three different solutions per the table below. All three share the same chitosan-glycerol base; only Group C adds polyphenols. Follow every step in order.

Group A

Negative Control

Distilled water only — no coating applied. Represents standard unprotected produce. Used as the baseline to measure all improvements.

Group B

Positive Control

1.5% Chitosan + Glycerol matrix only. No polyphenols. Isolates the physical barrier and elicitor effect of the biopolymer alone.

Group C

Active MVP

Full chitosan + glycerol + OMW polyphenol (hydroxytyrosol/tyrosol) nano-emulsion. This is the commercial product being validated.

Step-by-Step: Preparing Group B (Chitosan Base) — also applies to Group C

  • 1
    Make the Acetic Acid Solvent (1% v/v)

    Mix 1 part white culinary vinegar (≈5% acetic acid) with 4 parts distilled water. Example: 200 mL vinegar + 800 mL water = 1 litre solvent. This creates the acidic medium needed to dissolve chitosan.

    💡 Use a measuring cup with mL markings. Ratio is 1:4 (vinegar:water) by volume.
    Frontiers – Chitosan edible coating tuna filets (dissolution protocol)
  • 2
    Dissolve the Chitosan Powder (1.5% w/v)

    Weigh 1.5 g chitosan powder per 100 mL of solvent. Slowly add powder to the solvent while stirring. Set up a double boiler (bowl over pot of hot water) and heat to ~45°C. Whisk vigorously for 30–45 minutes until the solution becomes clear and honey-like.

    ⚠ Do NOT heat above 50°C — this degrades the polymer chains.
    Frontiers – Chitosan edible coating (preparation method)
  • 3
    Add Glycerol (Plasticizer)

    Add 0.25 mL food-grade glycerol per gram of chitosan used. Example: if you used 3 g chitosan → add 0.75 mL glycerol. Stir thoroughly. Glycerol prevents the dried coating from cracking or flaking.

    💡 Food-grade glycerol is available at pharmacies.
    PMC12030211 – Preparation and Application of Edible Chitosan Coating
  • 4
    Group C Only: Add OMW Polyphenol Concentrate

    Add your prepared OMW polyphenol extract (hydroxytyrosol/tyrosol fraction) to the chitosan-glycerol matrix. Blend immediately using a high-speed blender (Vitamix or equivalent) on maximum speed for exactly 5 minutes. This mimics the Phase Inversion nano-emulsification step.

    💡 The liquid should look uniformly greenish/amber with no oil separation.
    MDPI – Tyrosol-enriched tomatoes from chitosan coating
  • 5
    Rest & Deaerate

    Let the prepared solution stand undisturbed for 60 minutes to allow air bubbles to escape. Bubbles in the coating cause uneven coverage on the fruit surface.

    Louisiana Tech – Antimicrobial Edible Coating Chitosan (deaeration step)
Section 03

How to Apply the Coating

  • 1
    Randomize & Label Specimens

    Lay out all produce. Randomly assign each piece to Group A, B, or C and tag each with a number (e.g., "Strawberry-A-01"). This prevents bias from picking only "nice-looking" ones for the MVP group.

    💡 Write tags on sticky labels or masking tape. Keep a log matching numbers to groups.
  • 2
    Sanitize All Produce

    Wash all specimens in a dilute hypochlorite solution at 50 ppm for exactly 2 minutes. To make 50 ppm: add ~2.5 mL household bleach (2% NaOCl) to 1 litre of water. This removes pre-existing field fungi.

    Rinse thoroughly with distilled water afterwards to remove residual chlorine.

    ⚠ Group A (negative control): wash with distilled water ONLY, no coating steps below.
    Maxapress – Edible coatings for fresh produce: chitosan, beeswax (sanitization protocol)
  • 3
    Dry the Produce Completely Before Coating

    Place all specimens on sterilized wire cooling racks. Use a small fan for ambient air drying until surfaces are completely dry to the touch. Any water remaining will dilute the coating and prevent adhesion.

    ⚠ This step is critical — do not skip or rush it. Usually takes 15–20 min.
    Maxapress – Edible coatings for fresh produce (pre-drying protocol)
  • 4
    Immersion Coating (Groups B & C)

    Using sterilized stainless-steel tongs, submerge each specimen fully into its assigned coating solution. Hold submerged for exactly 3 minutes. On withdrawal, hold the specimen over the bowl for 15 seconds to allow excess to drip off, then place on the wire rack.

    💡 Keep specimens from touching each other on the rack to prevent coating tearing.
    Maxapress – Edible coatings for fresh produce (immersion technique)
  • 5
    Cure the Coating

    Leave coated specimens on the rack under gentle fan-assisted airflow at room temperature for exactly 60 minutes. The coating is fully cured when it transitions from a wet, shiny film to an invisible, dry-to-touch, semi-permeable membrane.

    💡 If the coating looks white or cloudy when dry, it's still too thick — that batch may cause anaerobic fermentation. Dilute slightly.
    Maxapress – Edible coatings for fresh produce (curing protocol)
  • 6
    Record Initial Weights (Day 0)

    Immediately after curing, weigh every individual specimen using a digital scale calibrated to 0.01 g precision. Record in your data sheet as W₀ (initial weight). This is your Day 0 baseline for weight loss calculations.

    Florida Online Journals – Weight loss and visual quality of fruits and vegetables
  • 7
    Distribute to Storage Conditions

    Split each group (A, B, C) of each fruit in half — one half goes to ambient storage, the other to refrigerated storage. Place in identical open-air plastic or glass trays. Specimens must not be wrapped or sealed — open-air exposure is required.

Section 08

Assessment Methods (Non-Destructive First)

Priority order: always do non-destructive (ND) methods first on every specimen, every assessment day. Destructive (D) methods only as last resort.

⚖️
1. Fresh Weight Loss % (Transpiration)
● Non-Destructive · Do First
  • Use a digital scale precise to 0.01 g. Weigh every specimen individually.
  • Record weight as Wₜ (weight at day t).
  • Calculate: WL(%) = ((W₀ − Wₜ) / W₀) × 100
  • W₀ = initial weight recorded on Day 0 (right after curing).
  • Plot on line graph over time. Coated specimens should show a shallower slope.
WL(%) = ( W₀ − Wₜ ) ÷ W₀ × 100
Florida Online Journals – Relationship between Weight Loss and Visual Quality of Fruits and Vegetables
👁️
2. Visual Decay Score (Senescence)
● Non-Destructive · Do Second
  • Score each specimen on the 1–5 scale (see Phase 2 guide for full scoring rubric with fruit-specific cues).
  • Always assess under the same artificial light (e.g., same lamp, same spot) to avoid daylight variation affecting colour perception.
  • Record score for each specimen individually, then calculate the group median and mode.
Florida Online Journals – Weight Loss and Visual Quality · PMC5903658 – Accuracy of visual evaluations of decay
🎨
3. Colour Evolution / Ripening Stage (Respiration Proxy)
● Non-Destructive · Do Third (Climacteric Fruits Only)
  • Cherry Tomato: Use the USDA 1–6 colour scale: 1=Green, 2=Breakers, 3=Turning, 4=Pink, 5=Light Red, 6=Red. Record the stage for each tomato.
  • Banana: Use the 1–7 Banana Ripeness Colour Index: 1=Full green → 7=Yellow with brown patches. A well-known visual reference.
  • If Group C tomatoes take 14 days to reach "Red" while Group A takes 7 days → respiration control is confirmed.
  • Take photos under consistent lighting on every assessment day.
ResearchGate – Ripening indexes for tomato colour stages · ASHS – Chitosan coating extend storage life of tomatoes
🦠
4. Fungal Decay Incidence (Binary 0/1)
● Non-Destructive · Visual Observation · Do Fourth
  • Inspect each specimen carefully. Record 0 = no fungal growth visible, 1 = any fungal growth visible.
  • Strawberry: Look for grey-white fuzzy coating → Botrytis cinerea (Grey Mould)
  • Cherry Tomato: Look for blue-green powdery spots → Penicillium expansum
  • Banana: Look for black sunken lesions → Colletotrichum musae (Anthracnose)
  • Bell Pepper: Look for grey-white fuzz or soft watery patches → Botrytis cinerea or Alternaria alternata
  • Calculate: Decay Incidence (%) = (number of specimens with score 1 ÷ total specimens in group) × 100
PMC12785490 – Active Edible Coatings to Mitigate Postharvest Diseases · UC Davis – Strawberry postharvest facts
💪
5. Firmness (Tactile + Equipment)
● Non-Destructive (with device) · Do Fifth
  • If you have a fruit firmness tester (penetrometer / durometer): Press the probe (8 mm tip) gently onto a flat surface area of the fruit. Record the force reading in kg or N. Take 2 readings per specimen (opposite sides) and average them. Do NOT break the skin.
  • Without equipment: Use the 1–5 Tactile Firmness Scale (defined in Phase 2 tool). Score by gently pressing with one finger — do not compress more than 2 mm.
  • Record firmness score for each specimen individually.
  • This is non-destructive as long as the skin is not punctured.
HIN – Portable firmness probe for fruit · NextGen – Fruit Firmness Tester guide
🔪
6. Destructive Penetrometry (Last Resort Only)
● Destructive · Only if no firmness equipment available · Do LAST
  • Only used if no firmness tester is available AND tactile scale alone is insufficient.
  • Use a rigid cylindrical probe (8 mm diameter tip). Place on fruit surface. Add calibrated weights (e.g., water in a cup on top) until skin yields.
  • Record the mass at yield. This specimen can no longer be used for subsequent assessments.
  • Strategy: Reserve 1–2 specimens per group solely for this test on the final assessment day.
⚠ Each specimen used for destructive testing is removed from the longitudinal tracking. Plan specimen numbers accordingly.
